Sparkling Wines

Champagne (France)

  • Non-Vintage Brut: Crisp, dry, citrus, green apple, toasty notes.
  • Vintage Champagne: Complex, rich, made from a single exceptional year.
  • Rosé Champagne: Elegant with strawberry and raspberry notes.
  • Blanc de Blancs: 100% Chardonnay, zesty and light.
  • Blanc de Noirs: Fuller-bodied, red fruit and brioche.

Prosecco (Italy)

  • Extra Dry: Slightly sweet, pear, apple, floral.
  • Brut: Drier, with green apple and citrus notes.

Cava (Spain)

  • Brut: Lemon zest, almond, green apple.
  • Reserva/Gran Reserva: More complex, nutty and toasty.

Other Sparkling Wines

  • Franciacorta (Italy): Traditional-method with fine bubbles.
  • Crémant (France): Affordable alternative, light and fruity.

White Wines

Light-Bodied Whites

  • Sauvignon Blanc: Citrus, grassy, minerally (France/NZ).
  • Pinot Grigio: Lemon, lime, green apple (Italy).
  • Albariño: Peach, citrus, slight salinity (Spain).
  • Vermentino: Herbal, citrusy (Italy).

Medium-Bodied Whites

  • Chenin Blanc: Apple, floral or tropical (France/SA).
  • Grüner Veltliner: Citrus, white pepper (Austria).
  • Soave (Garganega): Almond, pear, floral (Italy).

Full-Bodied Whites

  • Chardonnay: Citrus to tropical, oaky and buttery (France/USA).
  • Viognier: Peach, apricot, floral.
  • Roussanne/Marsanne: Honeyed, nutty, stone fruit (France).

Rosé Wines

Styles by Region

  • Provence Rosé (France): Pale, dry, strawberry, citrus, herbs.
  • Spanish Rosado: Bold, cherry, strawberry, spice.
  • Italian Rosato: Cherry, watermelon, herbal.
  • Tavel Rosé (France): Full-bodied, red berries, spice.
  • California Rosé: Raspberry, watermelon, floral.

Red Wines

Light-Bodied Reds

  • Pinot Noir: Cherry, cranberry, earthy (France, Oregon).
  • Gamay: Red berries, juicy, soft tannins (Beaujolais).

Medium-Bodied Reds

  • Merlot: Black cherry, mocha, soft (France/USA).
  • Sangiovese: Cherry, herbs, tomato leaf (Italy).
  • Zinfandel: Jammy, blackberry, cinnamon (USA).
  • Grenache/Garnacha: Raspberry, spice, pepper (Spain/Rhône).

Full-Bodied Reds

  • Cabernet Sauvignon: Blackberry, cassis, oak (USA/France).
  • Syrah/Shiraz: Plum, pepper, chocolate (France/Australia).
  • Malbec: Black cherry, cocoa, juicy (Argentina).
  • Tempranillo: Dried cherry, leather, tobacco (Spain).
  • Petite Sirah: Blueberry, pepper, intense tannins (USA).

Dessert Wines

***Sweet White Wines (Dessert Wines)***

Late Harvest Wines:

  • Late Harvest Riesling: Sweet with apricot, peach, and honey flavors.
  • Late Harvest Chenin Blanc: Rich, honeyed flavors with notes of citrus and pear.

Noble Rot (Botrytized Wines):

  • Sauternes (France): Rich and complex, with flavors of honey, apricot, and orange marmalade.
  • Tokaji (Hungary): Sweet with vibrant acidity, flavors of honey, orange, and nuts.
  • Beerenauslese (Germany): Lush and sweet, made from overripe, botrytized grapes, typically Riesling.

Muscat/Moscato:

  • Moscato d'Asti (Italy): Lightly sparkling with peach, apricot, and floral notes.
  • Muscat Beaumes-de-Venise (France): Sweet, with flavors of orange blossom, peach, and spice.

Other Sweet Wines:

  • Vin Santo (Italy): Sweet, nutty, and raisiny, often paired with biscotti.
  • Ice Wine (Canada, Germany): Intensely sweet with fruity flavors of peach, honey, and citrus.

***Fortified Wines***

Port:

  • Ruby Port: Sweet, full-bodied, dark berry flavors.
  • Tawny Port: Aged longer, with flavors of dried fruits, caramel, and nuts.
  • Vintage Port: Rich and complex with tannic structure.
  • LBV Port: Aged before bottling, with a smoother finish.

Sherry:

  • Pedro Ximénez (PX): Extremely sweet with raisiny, fig, and molasses flavors.
  • Cream Sherry: Sweetened Oloroso, with nutty and dark fruit flavors.
  • Amontillado: Medium-sweet with nuts, dried fruits, and caramel.
  • Fino: Dry, sometimes paired with nutty or citrus desserts.

Madeira:

  • Malmsey Madeira: The sweetest style, rich with caramel, nuts, and dried fruits, great for nut-based and rich cakes.
  • Bual Madeira: Sweet and full-bodied, with rich flavors of caramel and nuts, ideal for dark chocolate desserts.
  • Rainwater Madeira: Medium dry to medium sweet, with light citrus and nutty flavors, pairing well with citrus desserts.

***Other Sweet Wines***

  • Marsala (Italy): A fortified wine often used for cooking, but also available in a sweet style. It has flavors of caramel, vanilla, and dried fruit, great for tiramisu or fruit cakes.
  • Sherry (Sweet) Cream Sherry or PX Sherry are both excellent choices for very sweet and rich desserts.
  • Vermouth: Fortified and aromatized; sweet or dry, with complex flavors that pair well with a range of dishes.

***Other Dessert Wines***

  • Madeira: A fortified wine from the Madeira Islands, it comes in varying degrees of sweetness:
      • Malmsey: Sweet, perfect with rich, nutty, or caramel-based desserts.
      Bual: Rich, with dark fruit and caramel flavors, it pairs well with spice cakes and chocolate.

Glossary of Wine Terms

  • Tannin: A compound found in grape skins, seeds, and stems that gives wine its dry, astringent feeling.
  • Acidity: The tartness or freshness in wine, which helps balance sweetness and adds to the overall crispness.
  • Body: The weight or fullness of the wine, ranging from light to full-bodied, based on alcohol, tannins, and acidity.
  • Terroir: The unique combination of soil, climate, and grape variety that gives wine its distinct character.
  • Vintage: The year the grapes were harvested and made into wine.
  • Varietal: Wine made from one type of grape, like Cabernet Sauvignon or Chardonnay.
  • Blend: A wine made from more than one type of grape.
  • Old World: European wine regions (e.g., France, Italy, Spain).
  • New World: Non-European wine regions (e.g., USA, Australia, Chile).
  • Oak Aging: The process of aging wine in oak barrels, which can add flavors like vanilla, spice, and toastiness.

Key Terms:

  • Game Meat (Wild Game):

    - Wild animals that are hunted for food, rather than raised on farms. e.g: Venison (deer), wild boar, pheasant, duck, rabbit, and other wild birds or animals.

  • Old-World:

    - Refers to wines made in traditional wine regions of Europe (e.g., France, Italy, Spain). Typically, these wines have a more restrained, earthy, and less fruit-forward style.

  • New-World:

    - Wines made in regions outside of Europe, such as the United States, Australia, South America, and South Africa. New-World wines are often more fruit-forward and bold compared to Old-World wines.

  • Bordeaux Blend:

    - Refers to a wine made with a blend of grape varieties that are typically associated with the Bordeaux region in France. These wines are often made from a combination of Cabernet Sauvignon, Merlot, Cabernet Franc, Petit Verdot, and Malbec (for reds) or Sauvignon Blanc, Sémillon, and Muscadelle (for whites).

    - The Bordeaux Blend is a signature style in the region, where different grape varieties are mixed to balance the flavors, tannins, and acidity.

    - While the term "Bordeaux Blend" is most commonly used for wines from the Bordeaux region, it is also used globally to refer to blends made with these classic grape varieties, particularly in regions with a similar climate and terroir.

  • Meritage:

    - Is a term used in the United States to describe a specific blend of wines made from traditional Bordeaux grape varieties. The term is a combination of "merit" and "heritage," implying a high-quality blend rooted in Bordeaux traditions.

    - To be labeled as "Meritage," the wine must be a blend of at least two of the following grape varieties:

    • Red Meritage: Cabernet Sauvignon, Merlot, Cabernet Franc, Petit Verdot, Malbec
    • White Meritage: Sauvignon Blanc, Sémillon, Muscadelle

    - Unlike the term "Bordeaux Blend," which refers to blends from the Bordeaux region in France, "Meritage" is protected by a trademark and is used exclusively for wines made in the U.S.

    Game Meats:

  • Venison (Deer)
    • Description: Venison is lean, dark red meat with a robust, slightly sweet flavor. It’s often described as “earthy” and can be slightly gamey depending on the cut and preparation.
    • Wine Pairing: Full-bodied reds with good acidity and tannin structure complement venison well. Cabernet Sauvignon, Syrah, Zinfandel, and Malbec are excellent choices. The tannins help balance the richness and earthy flavors.
  • Wild Boar
    • Description: Wild boar is leaner and has a more intense, slightly sweet and nutty flavor compared to domestic pork. It can also be a bit fattier in some cuts.
    • Wine Pairing: Wines like Zinfandel, Sangiovese, or Barbera are great with wild boar, as their acidity and fruitiness can balance the gaminess and fat. Bold reds with some spice work particularly well.
  • Rabbit
    • Description: Rabbit meat is light, lean, and delicate in flavor, often described as somewhere between chicken and game. It’s slightly earthy but not as intense as venison or wild boar.
    • Wine Pairing: Lighter red wines like Pinot Noir or medium-bodied whites like Chardonnay (especially if oak-aged) work well with rabbit. For a more rustic pairing, Gamay or Grenache can also be a good match.
  • Pheasant
    • Description: Pheasant is a lean bird with a mild, slightly sweet, and earthy flavor. It’s richer than chicken but not as intense as duck.
    • Wine Pairing: A light to medium-bodied red like Pinot Noir or a white with some structure like Chardonnay (preferably one with some oak influence) complements pheasant nicely. If the pheasant is prepared with a richer sauce, a more full-bodied wine like Merlot or Zinfandel could also work.
  • Duck
    • Description: Duck is a fatty, flavorful bird with rich, dark meat and a slightly gamey flavor. It’s often cooked with a crispy skin or in rich sauces (like orange sauce or berry reductions), which can add complexity to the dish.
    • Wine Pairing: Pinot Noir is a classic choice due to its ability to pair with the richness and fat in duck. Other good choices include Zinfandel, Syrah, or even Shiraz, especially when the dish has a sweet component like fruit sauce or glaze.
  • Quail
    • Description: Quail is another small bird, typically lean with delicate meat. It has a mild, somewhat nutty flavor and is often roasted or grilled.
    • Wine Pairing: Light to medium reds like Pinot Noir, Gamay, or whites like Viognier work well with quail. If served with a sweet or fruity glaze, a wine like Zinfandel can also be a great match.

Game Meat Characteristics:

  • Leanness: Game meats are generally leaner than farm-raised meats, meaning they have less fat, which can make them drier and more intense in flavor.
  • Earthy, Robust Flavors: The wild diet of the animals results in stronger, sometimes gamey flavors that can be overwhelming if paired with delicate wines. Bold wines with structure are often preferred to stand up to these flavors.

Steak Doneness:

  • Rare
    • Internal Temperature: 120-125°F (49-52°C)
    • Description: Very red, cool center. The outside is seared, while the inside remains very raw and tender. Rare steaks are usually very soft and juicy.
  • Medium Rare
    • Internal Temperature: 130-135°F (54-57°C)
    • Description: Warm, red center. This is often considered the ideal doneness for steaks, providing a good balance of tenderness, flavor, and juiciness. The outside is seared, and the inside remains pink with a warm, red center.
  • Medium
    • Internal Temperature: 140-145°F (60-63°C)
    • Description: Pink center with a slightly firm texture. The steak is cooked through, but still retains some juiciness and flavor. The outside is browned and a bit more cooked.
  • Medium Well
    • Internal Temperature: 150-155°F (66-68°C)
    • Description: Slightly pink center with a firm texture. The steak is nearly fully cooked, with just a hint of pink inside. It may be a bit drier than a medium steak.
  • Well Done
    • Internal Temperature: 160°F+ (71°C+)
    • Description: Fully cooked with no pink, dry, and firm throughout. The outside is browned and charred, while the inside is grayish-brown, with very little moisture left.

Food Pairing Considerations for Doneness:

  • Rare and Medium Rare: Often paired with full-bodied red wines like Cabernet Sauvignon, Malbec, or Syrah. The bold tannins and rich fruit of these wines complement the richness of the meat.
  • Medium: Pairs well with a range of red wines, such as Merlot or Zinfandel, which balance the meat's richness without overpowering the flavor.
  • Medium Well and Well Done: Lighter red wines like Pinot Noir or Chianti, or even whites like Chardonnay, can work better with these levels of doneness since the meat tends to become drier and less juicy.

Wine Pairings for Tomato-Based Sauces

When tomato is the base sauce for a dish (such as in pasta, pizza, or other Italian-inspired dishes), wine pairings depend on the acidity and richness of the tomato sauce. Tomato-based sauces are typically tangy, with a balance of acidity, and they often have savory, herbal, and slightly sweet characteristics depending on the recipe.

1. Red Wines:

  • Chianti (Sangiovese)
    Region: Tuscany, Italy
    Chianti is a classic red wine from the heart of Tuscany, made primarily from the Sangiovese grape. It has high acidity, which makes it an ideal match for tomato-based sauces, especially Italian dishes like pasta or pizza.
  • Zinfandel
    Region: California, USA (also in parts of Italy, known as Primitivo)
    Zinfandel is known for its bold flavors of ripe fruit and spice, making it a great pairing with rich, tomato-based dishes like pizza or pasta with meat sauce.
  • Barbera
    Region: Piedmont, Italy
    Barbera is an Italian red wine, highly regarded for its bright acidity and fruit-forward character. It’s a versatile wine that works particularly well with tomato-based sauces, including pasta, pizza, or dishes with roasted vegetables.
  • Cabernet Sauvignon
    Region: Bordeaux (France), Napa Valley (California, USA), Coonawarra (Australia), and more
    Known for its full body and tannins, Cabernet Sauvignon pairs well with rich tomato-based dishes, especially those involving grilled meats, beef, or lamb.
  • Merlot
    Region: Bordeaux (France), California (USA), Chile, Australia
    Merlot is a softer, fruitier red wine compared to Cabernet Sauvignon. Its smooth texture and moderate acidity make it a good match for tomato-based dishes, especially when paired with roasted meats, pasta, or poultry.

2. White Wines:

  • Pinot Grigio
    Region: Veneto, Friuli Venezia Giulia (Italy), Oregon (USA), Alsace (France)
    This light and crisp white wine is known for its refreshing acidity and subtle flavors. It’s an excellent pairing for lighter tomato-based dishes, like seafood pasta or a light vegetable sauce.
  • Sauvignon Blanc
    Region: Loire Valley (France), Marlborough (New Zealand), California (USA)
    Sauvignon Blanc’s crisp acidity and herbaceous flavors make it an excellent match for tomato-based dishes that include herbs or vegetables, like a fresh tomato salad or pasta with pesto.
  • Unoaked Chardonnay
    Region: Burgundy (France), Sonoma Coast (California, USA), Australia
    An unoaked Chardonnay offers a refreshing crispness with flavors of green apple and citrus, which complement tomato sauces, particularly with lighter dishes like chicken pasta or seafood-based sauces.
  • Vermentino
    Region: Sardinia (Italy), Tuscany (Italy), Liguria (Italy), Corsica (France)
    Vermentino is an aromatic white wine with citrus and herbal notes that pair well with seafood or light vegetable-based tomato sauces. It’s a perfect choice for Mediterranean-inspired dishes.

3. Rosé Wines:

  • Provence Rosé
    Region: Provence (France)
    Provence is the birthplace of rosé, and wines from this region are known for their crisp, dry, and fruity character. Provence rosé has enough acidity to pair wonderfully with pasta, pizza, and Mediterranean dishes with tomato sauce.
  • Grenache Rosé
    Region: Rhône Valley (France), Spain (where it’s known as Garnacha), California (USA)
    Grenache rosé is light, fruity, and refreshing, making it a great pairing with tomato-based dishes that are slightly on the sweeter or spicier side, such as barbecue pizza or pasta with sausage.
  • Tempranillo Rosé
    Region: Rioja (Spain), Navarra (Spain), and other parts of Spain
    Tempranillo rosé brings berry fruit flavors along with a touch of spice. It pairs beautifully with tomato-based dishes like pizza or pasta with a rich tomato sauce or grilled vegetables.

Wine Pairings for Cream-Based Sauces

Cream-based sauces, with their rich, smooth texture, are a versatile foundation for many dishes. These sauces tend to be rich, buttery, and comforting, often pairing well with wines that can complement or cut through their creamy texture. Here are some excellent wine choices for cream-based dishes such as pasta Alfredo, chicken with cream sauce, or seafood with creamy dips.

1. Red Wines:

  • Pinot Noir
    Region: Burgundy (France), Oregon (USA), California (USA)
    Pinot Noir is a light red wine with soft tannins and fresh fruit flavors. Its bright acidity and subtle complexity make it a great match for creamy dishes, particularly those with mushrooms or chicken.
  • Gamay
    Region: Beaujolais (France)
    Gamay is known for its light body and fruity flavors, with mild tannins that complement the richness of cream-based dishes. It pairs wonderfully with poultry or dishes with a creamy mushroom sauce.
  • Merlot
    Region: Bordeaux (France), California (USA), Chile, Australia
    Merlot's smooth, fruit-forward flavors and moderate acidity make it an excellent choice for creamy pasta dishes or roasted meats with cream-based sauces, such as chicken or pork.
  • Zinfandel
    Region: California, USA (also in parts of Italy, known as Primitivo)
    Zinfandel’s bold flavors of ripe fruit and spice can balance the richness of cream sauces. It pairs well with dishes that feature hearty cream-based sauces, such as pasta with meatballs or creamy barbecue chicken.

2. White Wines:

  • Chardonnay
    Region: Burgundy (France), Napa Valley (USA), Sonoma (USA), Australia
    A classic choice for creamy dishes, Chardonnay, particularly unoaked or lightly oaked varieties, offers a refreshing acidity that can cut through the richness of cream-based sauces, such as Alfredo or lobster pasta.
  • Sauvignon Blanc
    Region: Loire Valley (France), Marlborough (New Zealand), California (USA)
    Sauvignon Blanc’s zesty acidity and citrus notes help balance the heaviness of cream sauces. It works especially well with creamy seafood dishes, such as crab or shrimp pasta.
  • Pinot Grigio
    Region: Veneto (Italy), Friuli Venezia Giulia (Italy), Oregon (USA)
    Pinot Grigio is light and crisp, making it a perfect pairing for lighter cream-based sauces, particularly those with seafood or chicken, without overwhelming the dish.
  • Viognier
    Region: Rhône Valley (France), California (USA), Australia
    Viognier is a rich, aromatic white wine with floral notes and a full body, which complements rich cream-based sauces. It pairs well with spicy creamy dishes, such as creamy curry or creamy chicken and vegetable dishes.

3. Rosé Wines:

  • Provence Rosé
    Region: Provence (France)
    Provence rosé is a dry, crisp wine with bright acidity, making it a versatile pairing for creamy pasta dishes, especially those with a light cream sauce and seafood.
  • Grenache Rosé
    Region: Rhône Valley (France), Spain (where it’s known as Garnacha), California (USA)
    Grenache rosé is light and fruity, providing a fresh contrast to the richness of cream-based sauces. It works well with Mediterranean-style cream sauces, especially with grilled vegetables or chicken.
  • Tempranillo Rosé
    Region: Rioja (Spain), Navarra (Spain), and other parts of Spain
    Tempranillo rosé offers berry fruit flavors and a touch of spice, making it a good match for cream-based dishes like creamy pasta or roasted meats with cream sauces.

Wine Pairings for Complex Dishes

Complex dishes often feature a range of ingredients and flavors, including rich proteins, various herbs, spices, and diverse textures. These dishes can vary greatly, from gourmet meat dishes to intricate vegetarian options. For such dishes, wine pairings need to complement the dish's complexity without overwhelming the flavors. The wines selected should have enough depth and structure to enhance the various components of the dish.

1. Red Wines:

  • Cabernet Sauvignon
    Region: Bordeaux (France), Napa Valley (USA), Coonawarra (Australia)
    Cabernet Sauvignon’s bold tannins and full body make it ideal for complex, hearty dishes such as steak with rich sauces, lamb with herbs, or braised meats. Its structure and depth balance the richness and diverse flavors in these dishes.
  • Syrah/Shiraz
    Region: Rhône Valley (France), Barossa Valley (Australia)
    Syrah, known as Shiraz in Australia, is a powerful red with deep, spicy, and smoky flavors. It pairs well with complex meat dishes, like slow-cooked beef short ribs, barbecued meats, or dishes featuring strong spices or herbs like rosemary and thyme.
  • Merlot
    Region: Bordeaux (France), California (USA), Chile, Australia
    Merlot's soft tannins and fruit-forward character make it a versatile option for complex dishes. It pairs beautifully with dishes featuring both red meats and rich sauces, such as duck breast or roast beef with mushrooms.
  • Château-Neuf-du-Pape (Grenache-based blend)
    Region: Rhône Valley (France)
    This French wine blend, based on Grenache, offers deep fruit flavors, earthy undertones, and spices that match well with complex Mediterranean dishes, such as roasted lamb, stews, and dishes with a blend of vegetables, meats, and herbs.

2. White Wines:

  • Chardonnay (Oaked)
    Region: Burgundy (France), Napa Valley (USA), Australia
    Oaked Chardonnay has a full body and complex flavors that pair wonderfully with rich and complex dishes. It is excellent with roasted chicken with creamy sauces, lobster with butter, or seafood pasta dishes that include both shellfish and rich cream.
  • Gewürztraminer
    Region: Alsace (France), Germany, USA
    Gewürztraminer’s aromatic profile with floral, spicy, and sweet notes makes it a great pairing for dishes that combine sweetness and spice, such as Thai or Indian curries, or roasted meats with fruity accompaniments like apples or prunes.
  • Riesling (Off-Dry)
    Region: Mosel (Germany), Alsace (France), Finger Lakes (USA)
    The slightly sweet and fruity notes of off-dry Riesling work well with spicy and complex dishes, like those with Asian spices or rich, spiced barbecue. It is also a good choice for dishes that balance sweetness and acidity, such as roast pork with apple glaze.
  • Sauvignon Blanc
    Region: Loire Valley (France), Marlborough (New Zealand), California (USA)
    Sauvignon Blanc’s high acidity and herbaceous character pair well with complex vegetarian dishes, such as grilled vegetable salads or pasta with pesto. It also complements dishes with strong herbs and citrus notes, like goat cheese or lemony fish dishes.

3. Rosé Wines:

  • Provence Rosé
    Region: Provence (France)
    Provence Rosé is light, crisp, and dry with fruity and herbal notes, making it an ideal pairing for Mediterranean-style complex dishes, such as grilled seafood, roasted vegetables, or dishes with a variety of fresh herbs like basil and thyme.
  • Tempranillo Rosé
    Region: Rioja (Spain), Navarra (Spain)
    Tempranillo Rosé’s bright berry flavors and smooth finish work well with complex dishes that feature grilled meats, paella, or even slightly spiced dishes like tapas.
  • Grenache Rosé
    Region: Rhône Valley (France), Spain (Garnacha), California (USA)
    Grenache Rosé’s berry fruit and soft tannins make it a great match for dishes with a wide range of flavors, such as roasted poultry, pork, or even dishes with a blend of sweet and savory ingredients like roasted chicken with caramelized onions and figs.
